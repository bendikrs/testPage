Software Strategy Analysis

This analysis explores integrated software solutions for your ~100-employee product development and manufacturing company in Kongsberg. The primary objective is to identify a tightly connected platform covering CAD, PDM, PLM, ERP, and machine shop operations, considering your current SolidWorks/PDM foundation and your query about Onshape.

Candidate Ecosystems

Given the goal of minimizing vendors and custom integrations, the best approach is to evaluate entire ecosystems. We've identified three primary strategies, including the cloud-native Onshape/PTC platform you inquired about, which contrast with the incumbent and other major players.

Strategy 1: Onshape/PTC

Focuses on agility and collaboration. Onshape provides the core CAD/PDM. This is tightly integrated with PTC's Arena (PLM) and Windchill. ERP and MES are typically handled by tightly-integrated cloud partners.

  • Type: Cloud-Native
  • Core: Onshape (CAD/PDM)
  • PLM: Arena or Windchill
  • ERP/MES: Partner-based (e.g., NetSuite, Fishbowl)

Strategy 2: Dassault Systèmes

Leverages your current SolidWorks investment. The integrated path is SolidWorks (CAD/ECAD) + SolidWorks PDM + ENOVIA (PLM) and, crucially, DELMIAworks (formerly IQMS) for a fully integrated ERP/MES.

  • Type: Desktop-Centric / Hybrid
  • Core: SolidWorks (CAD/PDM)
  • PLM: ENOVIA
  • ERP/MES: DELMIAworks (Single-vendor)

Strategy 3: Autodesk

A strong cloud-hybrid competitor. Fusion 360 integrates Mech CAD, ECAD, and CAM in one tool. This is paired with Fusion 360 Manage (PLM) and Prodsmart (MES). An ERP (like NetSuite) is a common, well-integrated partner.

  • Type: Cloud-Hybrid
  • Core: Fusion 360 (CAD/ECAD/CAM)
  • PLM: Fusion 360 Manage
  • ERP/MES: Prodsmart (MES) + ERP Partner

Summary & Recommendation

Based on your company's profile—multi-disciplinary, prototype-heavy, and agile—a cloud-native or cloud-hybrid platform offers significant advantages. Both the Onshape/PTC and Autodesk Fusion 360 ecosystems provide the tight integration and flexibility you need, especially for connecting mechanical, electrical, and software data in one place.

Onshape/PTC Strategy (Your Query)

This is a very strong contender. Onshape's pure-cloud CAD/PDM is excellent for collaboration, remote work, and reduces IT overhead. Its integration with PTC's Arena (PLM) is tight. The key challenge would be finding a cloud ERP/MES that connects as seamlessly as a single-vendor solution.

Why it's mentioned: It represents the most modern, agile, and integrated design platform (CAD/PDM/PLM) available. It directly addresses the "tight integration" challenge on the R&D side and is ideal for mixed Mech/Elec/Soft development.

Dassault Systèmes Strategy (Incumbent)

This is the most 'traditional' and lowest-risk path, building on your existing SolidWorks data and expertise. The key advantage is DELMIAworks, which is a highly-regarded, fully-integrated ERP/MES designed for manufacturing companies. This would give you a true single-vendor solution from design through to shop floor execution. The potential downside is a less flexible, desktop-centric design environment.

Autodesk Strategy (Cloud Competitor)

This is a direct competitor to the Onshape strategy. Fusion 360's main advantage is its all-in-one design tool, combining mechanical CAD, ECAD, and CAM. This is extremely powerful for a company with an in-house machine shop. Paired with Fusion 360 Manage (PLM) and Prodsmart (MES), it's a very compelling, tightly integrated ecosystem.

Final Thought

Your choice depends on your primary bottleneck.
If your main challenge is design collaboration (Mech/Elec/Soft) and agility, the cloud platforms (Onshape or Autodesk) are superior.
If your main challenge is a seamless design-to-manufacturing data flow with a single source of truth for BOMs, inventory, and shop orders, the Dassault (SolidWorks + DELMIAworks) path is the most direct, proven, single-vendor solution.

Webinar Spotlight: The Onshape-Arena Connection

Your request for a "tightly connected" platform directly relates to this summary of a recent session on the Onshape (CAD/PDM) and Arena (PLM) integration. The core concept is creating a single, cloud-native source of truth to connect design, operations, and suppliers, eliminating version confusion and manual data entry.

Core Philosophy: "Shift Left"

The integration is built on the "Shift Left" philosophy. This means moving critical conversations about cost, manufacturability, and compliance earlier in the design process, where the cost of change is low, rather than later, where changes are expensive and cause delays.

Design Sync: A Single Source of Truth

This continuous flow (shown below) eliminates version control issues by uniting engineering, procurement, and manufacturing around one accurate source of data. When an engineer updates a design in Onshape, the Bill of Materials (BOM) automatically syncs to Arena PLM, allowing procurement and suppliers to see accurate, real-time data.

Key Features & Workflow Highlights

Formal Release Process

The connection manages the formal release to production. Tasks are created in Onshape, data flows to Arena for team and supplier reviews (ECR/ECO), and is then formally released to manufacturing.

Change Management Dashboard

In Arena PLM, managers get a high-level dashboard to track all Engineering Change Orders (ECOs), monitoring what's in-progress, rejected, or approved.

Key PLM Features in Arena

  • Unified Categories: Part categories created in Onshape (e.g., "Capacitor," "Screw") are mirrored in Arena, allowing for automated part numbering.
  • Advanced BOMs: Arena can manage the full product BOM, including non-modeled items (like glue, packaging, or software) that aren't in the Onshape CAD.
  • Sourcing & Suppliers: Directly manage sourcing information, approved supplier lists (ASL), and supplier priorities. Suppliers can even be given direct (and limited) access to approve changes on parts they supply.
  • Supply Chain Intelligence: A new feature to find alternative parts based on compliance, stock, or risk data, crucial for avoiding delays.
  • Formal ECOs: Arena runs its own formal approval workflows. A release can be automatically stopped if critical tasks (like a DFM check) are not completed.
  • Compliance Data: Serves as the single source of truth for regulatory information (RoHS, ITAR, etc.) attached to components.
